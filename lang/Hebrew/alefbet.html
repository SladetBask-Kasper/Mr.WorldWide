Hebrew Alphabet (אלף בית עברית - Alef bet ivrit)

Is it a difficult script to learn?

The hebrew alphabet is sometimes treated as much harder than it actually is. The alphabet is called "Alef bet" or "Alif bet" depending on which sound you think the letter "י - Yod" that makes a y/i/j sound which might at first sound quite difficult to know which to use, but in reality the J & Y both represent the same sound, like the J in Julia, and so you could make it even easier to learn.

So in other words the character "Yod" makes either a y/i/e sound. "i" is similar to both so for most of the the pronounciation will come naturally. So don't worry about the two to four almost identical or in some cases (like with j/y) exactly identical sounds.

What does "Alef bet" mean?

So "Alef bet" is just the name of the first two letters of the alphabet. Just like our word in english "Alphabet" is just the name of the first to letters of the Greek alphabet (which the roman one is based upon): Alpha Beta.

Some important Niqqud (נִקּוּד‎)


What is Niqqud?

Niqqud is a way of being more specific about what letters make what sound. You might have seen some of the characters such as Ayin, Vav and Bet have a number of sound. Niqqud is the way we'll be able to tell the diffrence

Bet/Vet

The Second letter of the alphabet is a kinda tricky but also probably the easiest to remeber. It has two major pronounciations that you'll need to care about. Bet (בּ‎) and Vet (ב). If there is a dot in the middle it is always similar to a "B" sound. Otherwise, assuming the text has Niqqud, it is probably a "V" sound. If the text doesn't have Niqqud then there really is no other way of telling other than learning how it's pronounced.

Vav

Vav with a dot in the middle, on the left (וּ) and with a dot on top (וֹ‎) are the two importaint ones to know. If there is text with Niqqud and there is no dot on the Vav then it is probably making a "V" sound simply. If its got a dot on middle left (וּ) then it makes a U or Å sound. If it has a dot on top (וֹ‎) it makes an "O" sound.

Ayin

Ayin is probably one of the most difficult letters on this list. If only it was as in Yiddish where it more or less always makes an "e" sound. However, you might see all kinds of crazy Niqqud with Ayin. So two general rules of thumb, is that if there is a line under the Ayin, like in עֲ or עַ then it works like an "A" sound. And if there is dots under with no line, like in עֵ then it is usually pronounced as an "E" sound, or as an "I" sound, primarialy an "I" sound in other languages, which in english should be kinda like "ee" in for example "meet". It might be kinda wierd when i say the the sound for dots but no line is something like e/i/ee but i/ee are the same sound and e and ee aren't far from each other.

Sometimes in old texts, Ayin in general, but specifically with a line under it can, make a "G" sound. So for example, the Palestinian city of Gaza inside the Gaza strip would be spelled "עַזָּה‎" in Hebrew. This word would be pronounced in modern hebrew as "Azah". In old Hebrew it would be pronounced kinda like "G" and there would be an "a" sound added between Ayin and Zign. So it would be, in old Hebrew, pronounced as "Gazah". The Arabic language has it's own version of Niqqud and it has a specific way of writing Ayin with a G sound and the Arabic spelling of Gaza is with that Ġain (G sound Ayin), and that is why we spell it "Gaza" in the west, because it's a transliteration of the Arabic spelling.
